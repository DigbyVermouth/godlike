A Fresh Way to Think About "Broken Heart"
A "broke" horse is not ruined — it's redeemed. It has learned the joy of moving as one with its master. God doesn't want to break us; He wants to "break" us free.

Attitudes & Conditions to Avoid
These heart conditions appear repeatedly in scripture as barriers between us and God. They share a common theme: resistance, self-sufficiency, and refusal to yield.
Avoid
Stiff-necked
Stubborn, hard to lead. From the image of an ox that refuses the farmer's goad. Resists the Holy Spirit's redirection. Results in wandering — like 40 years in the wilderness.
Exodus 33:3 · Acts 7:51 · 2 Chronicles 30:8 · Deut. 9:6
Avoid
Hard-hearted
Emotional and spiritual callousness. The heart becomes unable to feel the Spirit's promptings. Often paired with "stiff-necked" — the outer stubbornness and inner unfeelingness work together.
Mark 6:52 · Hebrews 3:13 · Ezekiel 11:19 · 2 Chronicles 36:13
Avoid
Pride / Haughtiness
Feeling no need for God. "God is opposed to the proud, but gives grace to the humble." Pride creates spiritual distance — the proud person feels self-sufficient and misses God's guidance from afar.
1 Peter 5:5 · Psalm 138:6 · Proverbs 18:12 · Prov. 29:23
Avoid
Spiritual Blindness & Deafness
Uncircumcised in heart and ears — inwardly unresponsive. Unable to perceive spiritual things. Stephen's charge to those who rejected Christ: "You always resist the Holy Spirit."
Acts 7:51 · Isaiah 6:10 · John 12:40 · Matthew 13:15
Avoid
Murmuring & Ingratitude
Constant complaint signals distrust of God's love and plan. Murmuring was among the Israelites' most persistent failures. It closes the heart to gratitude, which is a key pathway to grace.
1 Corinthians 10:10 · Numbers 14:27 · Philippians 2:14
Avoid
Leaning on Own Understanding
Trusting exclusively in human reason rather than trusting God's wisdom. This is the opposite of the teachable spirit that invites revelation. Often subtle — we may not even notice we're doing it.
Proverbs 3:5-6 · Isaiah 55:8-9 · Jeremiah 17:5
Avoid
Vain / Ritual-Only Worship
Going through outward motions without a surrendered heart. God rebuked Israel: He desired a broken and contrite heart above burnt offerings. External compliance without internal transformation.
Isaiah 1:11-15 · Psalm 51:16-17 · Matthew 15:8 · Amos 5:21
Avoid
Discouragement / Self-Reliance in Failure
Discouragement is a hidden form of pride — it means we're relying entirely on ourselves. True humility means trusting God even when we fall short, rather than despairing at our own weakness.
D&C 123:17 · Joshua 1:9 · Philippians 4:13
Attitudes & Conditions to Seek
These are the conditions God promises to dwell with and respond to. Each is a form of opening — surrendering the natural man to make space for the divine.
Seek
Broken Heart (A "Broke" Heart)
Not devastation — but willing surrender of will. Like a tamed horse that has found joy in moving with its master. Open to the Spirit of God; recognizes total dependence on Him. Protects against temptation.
3 Nephi 9:20 · Psalm 51:17 · 2 Nephi 4:32 · Mosiah 4:12
Seek
Contrite Spirit
Hebrew: "crushed to dust." A willingness to totally submit to God's will, having crushed the natural man's instincts. No longer resisting the things of the Spirit but embracing them. "Godly sorrow" (2 Cor. 7:10).
2 Corinthians 7:10 · Isaiah 57:15 · Psalm 34:18 · D&C 20:37
Seek
Meekness
Not weakness — controlled strength. "Godfearing, righteous, humble, teachable, and patient under suffering." Mormon taught that without meekness, faith and hope are vain. Jesus himself claimed to be meek and lowly.
Matthew 11:29 · Moroni 7:44 · Galatians 5:22-23 · Numbers 12:3
Seek
Humility
Acknowledging dependence on God; not feeling small but making oneself small. "I dwell in the high and holy place, and also with the humble and contrite." God leads the humble by the hand and answers their prayers.
Isaiah 57:15 · Psalm 25:9 · D&C 112:10 · James 4:10
Seek
Trembling at His Word
Deep reverence and receptivity to scripture. "To this one I will look: he who is humble, contrite in spirit, and trembles at My word." Openness to being changed by what God says — not just informed.
Isaiah 66:2 · Ezra 9:4 · Psalm 119:120
Seek
As a Little Child
Trusting, teachable, without pretense. "Except ye be converted and become as little children, ye shall not enter the kingdom." This attitude believes, asks without embarrassment, and receives without pride.
Matthew 18:3 · 3 Nephi 9:22 · Mosiah 3:19 · Mark 10:15
Seek
Lowly of Heart
A deep inner poverty of spirit — not self-deprecation, but genuine dependence. The Beatitudes begin here: "Blessed are the poor in spirit." God regards the lowly; the haughty He knows from afar.
Matthew 5:3 · Psalm 138:6 · Isaiah 66:2 · Luke 1:52
Seek
Gratitude & Praise
A thankful heart is an open heart. Gratitude for Christ's suffering on our behalf is listed as a dimension of a broken heart. It softens us, redirects focus from self to God, and is itself an act of worship.
D&C 59:7 · Psalm 100:4 · 1 Thessalonians 5:18 · Col. 3:15
Daily Practices That Cultivate the Right Heart
Attitudes aren't just willed into existence — they're cultivated through consistent daily practice. These are the practices with the strongest scriptural and practical support for reshaping the heart over time.
Morning — Set the heart's direction
Sincere Prayer — Yielding, Not Requesting
Begin by acknowledging your dependence on God rather than launching into requests. The Hebrew word for "heart" in scripture means the inner man or will — so prayer is literally re-orienting your will toward God. Try beginning with: "I yield my will to Thee today."

Key posture: Not monologue but genuine listening. "Put difficult questions in the back of your minds and go about your lives. Ponder and pray quietly and persistently." (Boyd K. Packer)
Morning or Evening — Tremble at His Word
Scripture Study — With Intent to Be Changed
Read with the question: "What does God want me to do differently today because of this?" — not just for information but for transformation. Isaiah 66:2 says God looks to those who "tremble at my word." This is more than reverence — it's readiness to act.

Practical tip: Even 5 minutes of genuinely prayerful scripture reading outweighs 30 minutes of passive reading. Write one thing you noticed.
Weekly or Monthly — Deepen Dependence
Fasting — Submitting the Natural Man
Fasting is specifically designed to humble the body so the spirit can lead. After 24 hours without food, the body submits to the spirit — and sensitivity to the Holy Ghost increases. It trains the flesh to obey rather than dominate.

Key insight: "Fasting is not a way to get a better response to prayer. Rather, it is a means of fostering a humbler approach to prayer." (Cru) — It changes us, not God.

Principle: Begin and end with prayer; fast with a specific purpose.
Daily — Practice Surrender in Small Things
Repentance as a Daily Posture (Not Just Crisis Management)
Repentance isn't only for major sins — it's the daily practice of checking our hearts. Bishop Budge's four-word pattern from horse training: Desire → Inquire → Require → Retire.

Desire what God desires. Inquire of Him. Do what He requires. And retire — rest in His arms rather than running wild through life alone.

This is the "broken heart as daily shield" — Nephi prayed, "May the gates of hell be shut before me, because that my heart is broken and my spirit is contrite!" (2 Nephi 4:32)
Ongoing — Cultivate Meekness Through Others
Service & Forbearance
Meekness grows in relationship. Paul connects meekness to "bearing with one another in love" (Eph. 4:2). Serving others without seeking credit, listening rather than talking, honoring others above ourselves — these are the daily exercises that reshape pride into humility.

Connection: Fasting is also linked to compassion for others — Isaiah 58 pairs fasting with feeding the hungry and freeing the oppressed. Outward service and inward softening reinforce each other.
Key Insight — The Physical/Psychological/Spiritual Unity
The Body Matters: Posture, Breath, and Stillness
Stiff-necked and hard-hearted are described in scripture as physical, psychological, and spiritual conditions simultaneously. This means the body participates in the heart's posture. Kneeling, bowing the head, fasting — these physical acts aren't theater; they participate in cultivating the inner condition.

Experiment idea: Try noticing moments in prayer or study when your body stiffens, and consciously relax. Notice whether your heart follows.

Body–Mind–Spirit: The Stress–Hardness Connection
One of the most original angles of this talk: scripture's language of stiff necks and hard hearts is not merely metaphorical — it maps directly onto real physiology. Chronic stress literally hardens us, physically and spiritually. And disciplines from the Alexander Technique to martial arts have discovered the antidote.
The Central Connection
Stiff-necked is not just an ancient insult. It describes a real physical state — the startle reflex frozen in place — that shuts down our receptivity to everything, including the Spirit.
Connects Exodus 33:3 · Acts 7:51 · Alexander Technique research · Frank Pierce Jones, Tufts University (1976)
The Chain: Stress → Startle → Stiff-neck → Hard Heart
Step 1 — The Trigger
Chronic Stress Puts Us in Permanent Fight-or-Flight
When a threat is perceived, the hypothalamus triggers a cascade of hormones — adrenaline and cortisol — that raise heart rate, tighten muscles, and prepare the body to fight or flee. This response is designed to be temporary. But when stressors are always present and you always feel under attack, the fight-or-flight reaction stays turned on. The result: chronic stress induces sustained activation of the sympathetic nervous system, leading to elevated cortisol and epinephrine, promoting oxidative stress, inflammation, and dysfunction throughout the body.

Spiritual parallel: A person under chronic stress is physiologically primed to resist, contract, and shut down. This is the same internal state as "stiff-necked."
Step 2 — The Body's Response
The Startle Reflex — Stiff-Neck Made Visible
The startle pattern follows a specific sequence: the muscles of the neck contract, pulling the head back and jamming it down onto the spine. The ribs and shoulders tighten and narrow, the chest flattens, and the legs flex — all completed in about half a second.

The more anxious and stressed a person is, the more frequently they tend to over-react to situations with this pattern of flinching and contracting. Some people habitually live with their muscles held in a contracted manner, echoing their previous responses to alarming situations.

Key insight: Most people's habitual way of moving seems to resemble a permanent, modified startle pattern — which clearly interferes with their ability to move, breathe, think, or feel in an alert, integrated, healthy way. This is chronic stiff-neckedness — a body locked in resistance.
Step 3 — The Spiritual Cost
A "Stone Heart" — Spiritual Receptivity Shuts Down
Ezekiel 11:19 speaks of the "heart of stone" that God promises to replace with a "heart of flesh." A stone heart is the spiritual equivalent of a body locked in chronic startle — rigid, defended, closed to any input that might require change or vulnerability.

The startle/stress response suppresses the very faculties needed for spiritual receptivity: open attention, stillness, the capacity to feel, and the parasympathetic "rest-and-receive" state. You literally cannot be spiritually receptive while your nervous system is screaming danger.

The chain: Unresolved stress → habitual startle posture → contracted neck and chest → suppressed parasympathetic system → inability to feel the Spirit → spiritual hardness that feels like indifference but may actually be exhaustion and defended pain.
Step 4 — The Antidote
Soft Neck, Open Heart — The Parasympathetic Pathway
Controlled breathing can significantly affect the autonomic nervous system, particularly by engaging the parasympathetic branch — responsible for the body's rest-and-digest response. This activation counteracts the stress response mediated by the sympathetic nervous system.

Research shows that psychosocial factors like positive emotions, cognitive flexibility, religion and spirituality, life meaning, social support, and active coping are protective ways to boost the body's resiliency to cope with stress.

The pathway to openness: Practices that soften the neck and lengthen the spine — slow breath, prayerful stillness, kneeling, gentle movement — literally shift the nervous system into the state where the heart can be reached.
The Alexander Technique
📐 What Alexander Discovered
🔑
The Primary Control
Alexander showed that the mechanism determining the character of all reflex action lies in the reflexes governing the relation of the head to the neck. Freedom here enables everything else.
🧠
Stress Patterns as Habit
Fear, anxiety, fatigue, and pain all show postural changes similar to those in startle — there is a shortening of neck muscles which displaces the head. These become habitual.
✋
Inhibition — Choosing Not to React
The technique focuses on learning what NOT to do first — becoming aware of, releasing, and refraining from harmful habits of muscle use. It's unlearning, not doing.
🕊️
Spiritual Echo
"Choose to be quiet throughout your whole body, with particular reference to the head and neck." — Margaret Goldie, Alexander teacher. This is the body's version of "be still and know."
⚔️ Martial Arts — Shoshin (Beginner's Mind)
🌱
Shoshin — 初心
Shoshin (Japanese: 初心) means "beginner's mind" — having an attitude of openness, eagerness, and lack of preconceptions when studying, even at an advanced level.
🧱
The "Cement" of Expertise
As you advance in rank, the mental "cement" can begin to harden and set, becoming closed-minded. The more expertise you accumulate, the more you tend to block contradictory information.
🧒
Many Possibilities
Shunryū Suzuki: "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind there are few." The same contrast as "stiff-necked expert" vs. "teachable child."
✨
Senshin — Enlightened Mind
Senshin represents a state of spiritual awakening — transcending ego and achieving inner clarity. Physical techniques flow naturally without conscious thought or hesitation.
The Synthesis for Your Talk
A New Way to Frame the Physical + Spiritual Unity
"Stiff-necked" is not an ancient insult — it is an ancient diagnosis. It names a condition that science now maps precisely: a nervous system locked in threat response, incapable of the openness required to receive. The spiritual practices God commands — stillness, kneeling, deep breath, yielding — are not merely symbolic. They are the very actions that shift us out of the startle state and into the condition where we can hear, feel, and be changed.

Practice
The Soft Neck as Spiritual Practice
Consciously releasing the neck and shoulders during prayer is not self-help theater — it participates in shifting the nervous system into the parasympathetic state where spiritual receptivity is possible. The Alexander principle: "neck free, head forward and up" is the opposite of the startle pattern — and the opposite of stiff-necked.
Psalm 46:10 · 1 Kings 19:12 (still small voice)
Practice
Curiosity as a Spiritual Discipline
Shoshin — beginner's mind — is strikingly parallel to "as a little child." Both describe an approach to God that is open, eager, without preconceptions. The closed expert mind and the stiff-necked Israelite share the same root: "I already know what God will say, and I'm not sure I want to hear it."
Matthew 18:3 · 3 Nephi 9:22 · Isaiah 66:2
Research
Religion Lowers Cortisol — Science Confirms
Participants with higher religiosity, levels of forgiveness, and frequency of prayer showed lower cortisol responses to stress. The practices that open the heart spiritually also measurably reduce the physiological hardening that stress produces. The two pathways are one.
Isaiah 26:3 · Philippians 4:7 — "the peace of God which passeth all understanding"
Talk Application — The Experiment to Suggest
Before your next prayer, pause. Notice where your neck and shoulders are. Gently release them. Take one slow breath. Then begin. Notice whether anything is different. This is not yoga — it is the body participating in the spiritual posture of surrender.

15-Minute Talk Outline
A suggested structure based on your research. Click each section to expand. Total: ~15 minutes when delivered at a natural pace (approx. 1,500–1,700 words spoken).
Section 1 · ~2 min
Hook: The Heart Condition We All Share
▾

Open with a relatable moment of spiritual dullness — a prayer that felt hollow, scripture study that felt routine. Ask: What separates us from God? Is it behavior — or something deeper?

💡 Personal experiment angle: "I've been testing this on myself. I want to share what I found."

Introduce the talk's thesis: Scripture describes closeness to God not primarily as a behavior problem but a heart condition. And there's a daily practice that changes the condition.

Section 2 · ~3 min
The Closed Heart: Stiff-Necked & Hard-Hearted
▾

Explain the origin of "stiff-necked" — the ox that resists the farmer's goad. It's a vivid, funny, relatable image.

  • The ox isn't evil — just stubborn. So are we.
  • When God tries to redirect us (through promptings, scripture, life circumstances), stiffening our neck means we don't turn.

Acts 7:51 — "You always resist the Holy Spirit."

Then connect: hard-heartedness is the internal version of the same problem — the heart becomes calloused and stops feeling the Spirit.

💡 Rhetorical question: "When was the last time you felt the Spirit clearly? If it's been a while — what might have hardened?"

Briefly name the other closed-heart conditions: pride, murmuring, leaning on our own understanding.

Section 3 · ~3 min
The Open Heart: Broken, Contrite, Meek
▾

Use the "broke horse" analogy (Bishop Budge). The word "broke" describes a horse that has discovered the joy and freedom of moving as one with its master — not broken, but redeemed.

3 Nephi 9:20 — The Lord's new sacrifice: "a broken heart and a contrite spirit."

  • Broken heart = open; recognizes dependence on God; sacrifices pride in all its forms
  • Contrite spirit = Hebrew "crushed to dust"; the natural man's instincts have been surrendered
  • Meek = God-fearing, teachable, patient; meekness is not weakness — it's controlled strength

Isaiah 57:15 — "I dwell with the contrite and lowly of spirit in order to revive their heart."

💡 The reward is not just forgiveness — it's that God comes to dwell with you. That's a different life entirely.
Section 4 · ~4 min
The Daily Practice: How to Cultivate the Right Heart
▾

The critical insight: these heart conditions aren't achieved once and kept forever. They are cultivated daily. Hebrews 3:13 says to "exhort one another daily, lest any of you be hardened."

Three practices scripture consistently links to softening the heart:

  • Prayer with a yielded will — Not a wish list, but a daily re-orientation of the will toward God. "I yield my will to Thee."
  • Scripture with trembling — Not just reading, but receiving. Isaiah 66:2: God looks to the one who "trembles at my word." Ask: "What does God want to change in me today?"
  • Fasting — Specifically designed to humble the body so the spirit can lead. It changes us, not God. It's how we practice submitting the natural man.
💡 Personal experiment: share one specific thing you noticed when you tried any of these with intentionality this week.

Bishop Budge's four words: Desire → Inquire → Require → Retire. Desire what God desires. Ask Him. Do what He asks. And rest in His arms — stop running wild.

Section 5 · ~3 min
Close: The Invitation & Promise
▾

Return to the opening image. The distance we feel from God is usually not His withdrawal — it's a heart condition we have developed, often gradually, through hardening.

The invitation of the gospel is always: yield. Turn. Give God the reins.

2 Nephi 4:32 (Nephi's personal prayer) — "May the gates of hell be shut before me, because my heart is broken and my spirit is contrite." This is the daily shield.

Close with a specific invitation to the congregation — choose one practice for the coming week:

  • Begin each prayer by yielding your will before making requests
  • Read one scripture passage slowly enough to feel it — and write one change it invites
  • Fast next fast Sunday with a specific heart-softening purpose

Micah 6:8 — "What does the Lord require of you but to do justice, love kindness, and walk humbly with your God?"

💡 Testimony: share what this practice has cost you and what it has given you.
